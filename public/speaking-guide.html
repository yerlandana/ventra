The Test

How IELTS Speaking works

One examiner, one candidate, 11–14 minutes, recorded. It is a real conversation — there are no "right answers", only how well you speak. You are scored on four equal criteria, not on your opinions.

Part 1 — Interview · 4–5 min

Familiar topics about you: home, work/study, hometown, hobbies, food, weather. Short, natural answers (2–3 sentences).

Part 2 — Cue Card · 3–4 min

You get a card with a topic. 1 minute to prepare and make notes, then talk 1–2 minutes without interruption.

Part 3 — Discussion · 4–5 min

Abstract questions linked to the Part 2 topic. The examiner pushes you to explain, compare, speculate and justify opinions.

Golden rule: never give one-word answers. Always answer → give a reason → add an example or detail. That single habit lifts most candidates from Band 5.5 to Band 6.5+.

Part 1 · Interview

Answering personal questions naturally

Part 1 warms you up. Keep answers relaxed and extended — two or three sentences. Don't memorise: examiners spot rehearsed speeches instantly and it lowers your Fluency score.

🏠 Common topics

  • Hometown & where you live
  • Work or studies
  • Free time & hobbies
  • Food, weather, travel

⏱️ Answer length

  • 2–3 sentences per answer
  • Answer → reason → example
  • Don't ramble past ~20 seconds

🎯 What's tested

  • Fluency (no long pauses)
  • Natural everyday vocabulary
  • Correct simple + past tenses

Model answers — see the structure

Q

"Do you enjoy cooking?"

Answer + reason + example, in a natural voice.

"Actually, I do — I find it quite relaxing after a long day. I'm no expert, but I love making pasta from scratch, and there's something satisfying about eating a meal you've made yourself."
Q

"What do you do in your free time?"

Give a couple of activities and a reason — avoid a bare list.

"It depends on my mood, to be honest. If I've got energy I'll go for a run, but more often than not I just curl up with a good book — it's my way of switching off."
Q

"Has your hometown changed much?"

Use a past-to-present comparison — shows tense range.

"Enormously. When I was younger it was a sleepy little town, whereas now it's grown into quite a busy city with cafés and shopping centres popping up everywhere."
DO
Extend every answer with a reason
Use natural fillers: "to be honest", "actually", "I'd say"
Sound interested — vary your intonation
Correct yourself smoothly if you slip
DON'T
Give one-word "Yes / No" answers
Recite memorised paragraphs
Speak in a flat, robotic tone
Say "I don't know" and stop
Part 2 · Cue Card (Long Turn)

The 1-minute plan & 2-minute talk

This is where most marks are won or lost. Use your 1 minute of prep to note keywords for each bullet — not full sentences. Then speak for the full 2 minutes: it is better to keep going than to finish early.

Cue-card structure — turn 4 bullets into a talk

~2 min · 220–280 words
Open — restate + choose

"I'd like to talk about [the thing]. The one that springs to mind is [specific example]…"

Bullet 1 & 2 — the facts (what / where / who)

Cover the descriptive bullets first: who, what, when, where. Add one small detail each.

Bullet 3 — the story / detail

Expand the "explain / describe" bullet — this is where you show off tenses and vocabulary. Give a mini-narrative.

Close — why it matters (the feeling bullet)

End on the "and explain why…" bullet with an opinion or emotion. This gives a natural finish if time runs out.

Prep-note trick (your 1 minute)

1

Pick fast, don't hunt for the "perfect" answer

Any real memory works. Choose in ~5 seconds so you have time to plan.

2

Write ONE keyword per bullet

Four bullets → four words. They trigger sentences; full sentences waste your minute.

e.g. card "a place you visited" → beach · summer · family · calm
3

Add 2 "wow" words

Note two strong phrases you want to use — a collocation or idiom — so they don't slip your mind.

Full sample Band 7+ long turn

Cue card: "Describe a place you visited that you found memorable. You should say where it is, when you went, who you went with, and explain why it was memorable."

Sample — a memorable place

~2 min · Band 7
Open — restate & choose

"I'd like to talk about a small coastal village called Kabak, in the south of Turkey. The first thing that comes to mind whenever someone mentions a memorable trip is that place."

Bullets — where / when / who

"I went there about three summers ago with a couple of close friends. It's tucked away at the bottom of a steep valley, so you have to hike down through a pine forest to reach the beach — which is exactly what makes it feel so untouched."

Detail — the story

"What struck me most was the silence. There was no phone signal, no traffic — just the sound of waves. I'll never forget waking up in a little wooden cabin and watching the sun come up over the sea. We spent the days swimming and the evenings sitting around a fire."

Close — why it matters

"All in all, it's somewhere I really treasure, because it was the first time I'd properly disconnected from everything. It reminded me how much I value simplicity — and that's basically why it's stayed with me ever since."

Part 3 · Discussion

Handling abstract questions

Part 3 is the hardest — and the biggest chance to reach Band 7+. Questions get abstract ("Why do people…?", "Do you think…?"). Don't answer in one line: use the P-E-E structure — Point, Explain, Example.

Point
State your opinion clearly. "I'd say that, on the whole, …"
Explain
Give the reasoning — the "because". "This is mainly because…"
Example
Ground it in reality. "For instance, in my country…"
Extend
Compare, speculate or concede. "That said, it might be different for…"
Q

"Why do you think people enjoy travelling?"

"I'd say there are a few reasons. Mainly, people crave a break from routine — a change of scenery recharges them. For instance, a friend of mine saves all year just to travel, because she says it's the only time she truly switches off. That said, some travel purely to learn about other cultures rather than to relax."
Q

"Do you think tourism can harm local communities?"

"It can, yes — it's a double-edged sword. On one hand it brings in money and jobs; on the other, mass tourism can drive up housing costs and erode local traditions. Venice is often cited as a place where locals have been priced out. So while the economic benefits are real, they need to be managed carefully."

Scoring

The 4 criteria — and how to climb them

Your Speaking band is the average of four equally-weighted criteria. Know exactly what each one rewards, and you can target the weakest.

🗣️ Fluency & Coherence

  • Speak at length without long pauses
  • Link ideas smoothly (and, but, so, because)
  • Self-correct without stopping the flow

📚 Lexical Resource

  • Range of topic vocabulary
  • Paraphrase instead of repeating
  • Some idioms used naturally

🧱 Grammatical Range

  • Mix simple & complex sentences
  • Use conditionals, relative clauses, a range of tenses
  • Errors don't block meaning

🔊 Pronunciation

  • Clear, easy to understand
  • Natural word & sentence stress
  • Vary intonation — don't sound flat

The band ladder — what each level sounds like

5

Band 5 — Modest

Keeps going but with noticeable pauses and repetition. Simple vocabulary, frequent basic errors. Understandable but effortful.

6

Band 6 — Competent

Willing to speak at length, though coherence sometimes slips. A mix of sentence forms; some errors but meaning is clear.

7

Band 7 — Good

Speaks fluently with only occasional hesitation. Uses less common vocabulary and some idioms; a range of complex structures with good control.

Target: extended answers, natural fillers, one idiom per topic.
8

Band 8 — Very good

Fluent and effortless, wide idiomatic range, precise word choice, flexible grammar with only rare slips, natural stress and rhythm.
